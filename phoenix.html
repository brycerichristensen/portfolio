Phoenix Payload
(NRG Battery)

As a Payload Engineer for BYU’s High Power Rocketry team, I am developing a 3U CubeSat payload for the 2026 SDL Payload Challenge. This project addresses the critical limitations of traditional chemical batteries in space—specifically cyclic fatigue, thermal degradation, and radiation sensitivity.
Our solution, Phoenix, stores energy within Negator Rotational Generator (NRG) cells. By utilizing strain energy in compliant mechanisms rather than chemical reactions, the Phoenix payload offers a thermally resilient, long-life power alternative capable of withstanding the extreme environments of deep space. This system is scheduled to flight-validate at IREC 2026, launching to 30,000 feet at Spaceport America.

The NRG Cell Architecture

The core of the system is the NRG cell, which utilizes 301 Stainless Steel negator strip springs wrapped in a planetary configuration. As a motor winds the cell, the springs deflect from the 'planets' onto a central 'sun' gear, storing energy as mechanical strain. Because 301 Stainless Steel does not undergo a ductile-to-brittle transition at low temperatures and resists creep up to 400°C, the NRG cell remains operational in temperature swings that would cause traditional batteries to fail.

Rapid Prototyping & Validation

A key advantage of the NRG architecture is its modularity. Much like chemical cells, individual NRG units can be arranged in series or parallel to tune voltage and current output. During the prototyping phase, we utilized high-precision 3D printing and FEA to optimize geometries, ensuring the system could be scaled to meet mission-specific power, volume, and weight requirements without redesigning the fundamental storage logic.

Performance Characterization

To convert stored strain into usable power, the NRG cell's ratcheting mechanism is released, allowing the springs to rewind and backdrive the motor to generate an electrical current. Our torque testing campaign (shown right) focuses on characterizing the torque-displacement curves to calculate exact energy density.

Data-Driven Optimization

These ground tests provide the baseline data needed to verify power output efficiency when the system deploys at apogee during its 30,000 ft flight test.

Future Plans & Flight Validation

By eliminating the common failure modes of chemical storage, the Phoenix payload demonstrates a path toward significantly extended mission lifetimes. Whether as a primary source for deep-space probes or a robust supplement for satellites in Low Earth Orbit (LEO), the NRG cell offers a level of thermal resilience and shelf-life that traditional systems cannot match.
