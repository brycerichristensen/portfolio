Phoenix Payload
(NRG Battery)

As a Payload Engineer for BYU’s High Power Rocketry team, I am developing a 3U CubeSat payload for the 2026 SDL Payload Challenge. The goal of this payload is to provide a proof-of-concept design for an alternative to traditional energy sources in space. Phoenix stores energy using custom-designed negator rotational generator (NRG) springs, which are released to generate power. This payload will launch to 30,000 feet in June at IREC (Spaceport America).

The NRG Cell Architecture

The core of our power system is the Negator Rotational Generator (NRG) cell. This building block consists of constant-force negator strip springs wrapped around a planetary gear configuration. As the cell is wound by a motor, the springs deflect around a central "sun" cylinder, storing energy in the form of mechanical strain. This approach eliminates the failure modes associated with chemical storage in extreme space environments.

Rapid Prototyping & Validation

To validate the mechanical logic of the compliant mechanisms, we utilized high-precision 3D printing to create functional physical models. These prototypes allowed us to verify the ratcheting system’s ability to maintain spring deflection for energy storage and ensure smooth transition logic during the rewind phase, where strain energy is converted back into electrical current.

Performance Characterization

Rigorous testing was performed using a custom torque tester to characterize the energy density of the Stainless Steel 301 springs. Because this material resists creep up to 400°C and maintains ductility at cryogenic temperatures, the NRG cell is uniquely suited for deep-space applications where traditional batteries require heavy thermal regulation systems.

Data-Driven Optimization

The results from our torque testing (shown right) provided the necessary data to verify the efficiency of the mechanical-to-electrical conversion. By analyzing the torque-displacement curves, we optimized the planetary geometries to maximize power output. This data serves as the baseline for our projected power generation during the 30,000 ft flight test.

Future Plans & Flight Validation

The Phoenix payload represents a robust, long-life supplement to traditional satellite power systems. Following our launch at IREC, we will log power output data to compare flight performance with our ground tests. Our subteam is currently focusing on final structural integration to ensure the 3U assembly withstands the extreme forces of rocket ascent.
