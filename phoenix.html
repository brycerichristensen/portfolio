Phoenix Payload

(NRG Battery)

As a Payload Engineer for BYU’s Rocketry team, I am developing a 3U CubeSat payload for the 2026 SDL Payload Challenge. This project centers on a mechanical battery utilizing a compliant mechanism and negator springs to store energy, serving as a concept demonstration for mechanical energy storage in space environments.

Concept & Base Building Blocks

The development began with defining the core storage unit. The "NRG" building block utilizes a specific spring geometry designed for constant torque and energy density. By starting with this base unit, we could iteratively test the mechanical properties before scaling up to the full 3U payload assembly.

Prototyping & Physical Modeling

LOREM IPSUM: To validate the CAD models, we moved into rapid prototyping using high-precision 3D printing. This physical model allowed the subteam to verify the compliant mechanism's movement and ensure that the ratchet and pawl systems engaged correctly under initial tension.

Torque Testing & Verification

LOREM IPSUM: Rigorous testing was performed using a custom torque tester. This setup allowed us to measure the exact energy input versus output, ensuring the compliant mechanism handled the spring's force without deformation or failure during repeated cycles.

Data Analysis & Results

LOREM IPSUM: The resulting data from the torque tests provided a clear picture of the system's efficiency. By analyzing the torque-displacement curves, we identified areas for geometric optimization in the pawls to further reduce friction and improve energy retention.

Future Plans & Flight Integration

LOREM IPSUM: Looking forward to the 2026 SDL Payload Challenge, the team is focusing on final material selection and structural flight integration. The goal is to demonstrate that this mechanical battery can reliably provide power to secondary systems in a CubeSat form factor during launch and orbit.
