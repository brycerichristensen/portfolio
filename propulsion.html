Propulsion Control System

I’ve been leading the controls team for BYU Propulsion Research since Spring 2025. My focus has been on improving the reliability of our static fire systems and designing the hardware/software architecture for our first flight configuration, launching in 2026.

Static Configuration

Noise Mitigation & DAQ Refinement

The previous DAQ system suffered from significant sensor noise. I led the effort to resolder pressure transducer protoboards, replace screw terminals with quick-disconnect soldered outlets, and upgrade the voltage regulation system using a buck converter and capacitor array. These improvements effectively stabilized our signal floor for higher-fidelity data.

System Timing & Reliability

I modified the LabVIEW control logic to correct critical timing issues between ignition and run-valve actuation. Additionally, I expanded the system's sensing capability from three to five pressure transducers and replaced bulky ignition switches with high-power MOSFETs, making the control box more compact and reliable.

Data Analysis

The graph on the right shows pressure transducer data from a recent hot-fire. PT1 tracks the oxidizer tank pressure drop over the 10-second test. These high-quality results allowed us to calculate combustion efficiency and refine our engine parameters for the next iteration.

Flight Configuration

Software Architecture

For the 2026 flight, I developed a comprehensive Finite State Machine (FSM) implemented in C++. This logic controls the propulsion unit, ground station, and fill trailer simultaneously, ensuring safety-critical transitions during the fueling and launch sequences.

Hardware Integration

Our flight hardware utilizes ESP32 microcontrollers communicating via SPI with thermocouples and PTs. I am currently developing the custom ground station UI to monitor real-time telemetry and manage the wireless communication links between the rocket and the fill trailer.
