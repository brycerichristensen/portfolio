Pipsqueak Engine

I built the pipsqueak air engine over the course of two classes. In my CAD and GD&T class I designed, modeled, and created engineering drawings for the assembly. Then, in my manufacturing processes class, I used various machining techniques to manufacture the engine. You can watch it in action below!

CAD Modeling & Design

I started by modeling the entire assembly in SolidWorks. The design features a simple oscillating cylinder mechanism where the cylinder itself pivots to open and close the intake and exhaust ports, eliminating the need for a complex valve train.

Technical Drawings

I then created detailed engineering drawings for each custom component. I focused on proper tolerancing and GD&T to ensure that the moving parts would maintain a tight seal while still allowing for smooth rotation.

Bill of Materials (BOM)

To prepare for manufacturing, I developed a complete Bill of Materials. This included both raw stock selection, like the uprights and cylinder, and off-the-shelf parts, like the screws and springs required for the tensioning mechanism.

Machining and Final Assembly

I manufactured the engine using a variety of techniques, including manual machining (mill, lathe), casting, and powder metallurgy. The flywheel was sand-cast in the foundry on campus, the crank wheel was pressed and sintered using copper powder, and most of the other parts were manufactured using manual mills and lathes.
